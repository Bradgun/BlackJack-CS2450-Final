Chapter 1: The Basics

The Premise

You, the player, can draw cards to get as close to the value of 21 as possible WITHOUT going over it.

The Deal

Players will place a bet amount of their choice prior to the game’s beginning. The Dealer will then shuffle a standard deck of 52 cards containing King, Queen, Jack, 2 through 10, and Ace cards for each of their respective suits: Spades ♠️, Hearts ♥️, Clubs ♣️ , and Diamonds ♦️. After this, the dealer will pass cards around the table one at a time until everyone has 2 cards[1].

The Gambit

After the Dealer has dealt the cards to each player, the game can now begin, and the Dealer will reveal one of their two cards, the card on the left (facing the players). Players are then allowed to view their own cards. Sequentially, each player can choose to “Hit” or “Stand”. “Hitting” allows the player to draw an additional card given to them by the dealer. “Standing” means that the player declares they no longer wish to draw cards and “stand” on their current accumulated value.

The Reveal

After all players stand, the Dealer then reveals their second card. The Dealer must continue to draw cards until they reach a minimum accumulated value of 17. After this, the game is over, and the results are checked. If you have a value greater than the Dealer without going over 21, you win! The payout for winning is 2-to-1 by standard, meaning that you get 2x the bet you placed down before the game if you win. If you have a value that’s the same as the Dealer, that’s what is known as a “Push”, which is essentially a tie, and your money is returned to you unchanged. Otherwise, if you have a value less than the dealer, or have “busted”, meaning that you exceeded the accumulated value of 21, you lose your bet for that round.

The Name of the Game

If you’re wondering where the game gets its name, it refers to a specific event that can occur upon the initial dealing of the cards. If a player happens to draw two cards that result in an accumulated value of 21, that is what is known as a “Blackjack”! This means that that player automatically wins the round! Blackjacks can occur when a player is dealt a face card (King, Queen, or Jack) or 10, alongside an Ace card (worth 11 for that scenario).

[1]: If you are the only player, the draw will alternate between you and the Dealer.

The Dealer’s Mechanics

Dealer Busting

If the Dealer manages to bust during their turn to draw after everyone else has stood, then any players who have not busted themselves win! However, for players who have busted, they unfortunately still lose their money, that’s the way the rules are.

Dealer Blackjack & Insurance

Now, what happens if the Dealer gets a Blackjack? This, of course, means that the Dealer automatically wins… BUT, there are precautions that the player can make to soften the loss. If the revealed card by the Dealer prior to the gambit shows an Ace (and only an Ace card enables this event), the Dealer is allowed to “peek” at the second card, and then ask each player if they would like to take “Insurance”. What Insurance is, is essentially a second bet the player can make that’s based on whether or not the Dealer has the Blackjack. If the Dealer does have the Blackjack, the players who have placed Insurance get their “Insurance Bet” back 2-to-1, like the standard game. However, it must be noted that Insurance Bets can only be up to half of the player’s original bet. If the Dealer does not have a Blackjack, then the player loses their Insurance bet.

Payouts & Additional Mechanics

Payouts

For standard hands that continue to draw until the player stands, the payout for the player is 2-to-1, as mentioned earlier, meaning that they are returned 2x their original bet.

For Blackjacks, the payout for the player (is usually) 3-to-2, meaning that the player is returned 1.5x their original bet. If that sounds unfair, this is how the House keeps their edge, in fact, some casinos even have payouts that are only 6-to-5, meaning the player only gets a 20% profit from a Blackjack hand.

Splitting

In the scenario that, in the opening deal, you are dealt two cards that have the same value[2] (i.e. 8 and an 8, or Queen and a Queen), you may choose to split your cards. What “Splitting” does is “Split” your hand into two hands, using each of the same cards as the starting card for each hand, and placing an equal bet to your original bet onto each of the hands. This means that, for example, if you have two 3s, choosing to split will move your original hand of two 3s being worth 6 total, into two hands each being worth 3, allowing you to hit and stand on each of your respective hands, each with its own bet.

Double Down

After the initial cards are all dealt, you can choose to “Double Down”, this means that, you can choose to double your bet, but you will only be able to draw one card after declaring it, and this can only be done on your first turn to draw (not including the initial deal).

Split and Double Down Interactions

For either of the listed mechanics above, you are only allowed to do one at a time, meaning you can’t both split and then double down on either hand, you can only choose one of the two techniques.

[2]: While Kings, Queens, and Jacks are all actually worth 10, the game still treats them in regards to their Written values, so, for example, you are not allowed to split a King and a Queen, even though they are both actually worth 10.

Strategies

Don’t Split 10s

It’s not recommended by experienced players to split 10s, this is because 20 is already a very solid value to stand on, better to do this rather than take an unnecessary risk of busting on two hands.

Don’t Split 5s

While it might be tempting to split 5s, it’s not recommended either because 10 is a great value to be at as it allows you to draw any card without risk of busting AND if you draw an Ace, you’ll be set.

Identifying Hard and Soft Hands

Hard hands refer to hands that have no Aces, meaning that their value cannot be changed to be in your favor. Soft hands are the opposite. Soft hands are generally more useful as it provides greater room for error.

For example, if you have drawn an Ace and a 5, it will be worth 16, which in of itself is not a sound stopping place considering the dealer will always draw until they reach at least 17. Choosing to hit with this hand, you would need to draw a card that’s a value of 5 or less without going over. But, since you have the Ace card, you can change the card’s value from 11 to 1, allowing you to draw again should you draw a card that’s 6 or more.

Hitting on Hard 16s

While this may seem counterintuitive, it’s actually better to hit on a hard-handed 16. The dealer must draw until they have at least 17, so it’s in your best interest to try rather than not, because either way, if the dealer draws to a good position, you’d have a 100% chance of losing if you stood, but a <100% chance if you tried. After all, there still is a 5/13 chance you can draw something that’ll work for you.

Stand on Anything at or Over 17

Counting Cards[3]

A technique used by real players in real-life casinos is to keep track of how many cards are remaining in the deck after each event. Events including the initial draw and each hit. While this seems like a rather futile endeavor, there actually is some merit to it, as it can slightly tip the scales in your favor if you’ve been keeping track. Also, it allows players to know whether or not the Dealer’s being honest about this deck that’s been dealt to them.
